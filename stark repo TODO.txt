Jose Friction
-------------
	* It seems that we have less "line search doesn't descend" errors. This might be the confirmation that the problem is less indefinite.
		This results in larger time steps.
	* It might work to the point that it improves convergence in the wrap sim by having less sliding around.
	* It is more faithful to Coulomb's friction.
	* We can cover discontinuous stick/slip conditions
		- We make them continuous with a positive parabolic transition
	
	* We can definitely further improve this method (by far) with the same qualitative result by keeping the categorization constant during a time step.
		The current mode supports arbitrary transitions from stick to slip and viceversa at any time.
		This is not only unnecesary but very detrimental to convergence.
		The problem is that the border between stick/slip is *very* close to zero (~1e-6 m)
		With all the other energies going on in the system, it is possible that Newton suggests something that moves the points more than that.
		Consequently, a lot of dofs are going to change gradient and hessians, potentially very significantly.
		But there is no real reason to do this except for specific types of simulations.
		
		In the car simulation for instance, the contact points between the tires and the floor either stick or slip, but they don't change all the time.
		When the car starts driving (without wheel spin) the contact points will have a huge fn, that together with the very large mu, will be a very clear indicator of no-slip condition.
		As the car drives slowly it will be very clear that we want the points to stick.
		It is very undersireable to waste time letting Newton bounce between states.
		
		When there is a sudden acceleration or turn, the wheels lose traction and the car spins.
		But this is also very easy to detect: there is a contact force yield point that switches from sticking to sliding friction.
		This is nothing more than `when friction force norm > threshold  ->  sliding constraint`.
		A friction force with a fixed static friction stiffness has a corresponding tangential displacement u if we prefer to use that instead.
		For the opposite, we check the sliding velocity/displacement of a contact point.
		If it is below a threshold, we treat it as a static friction case again.
		If displacement/velocity is not good enough, we can inspect the contact force and apply Coulomb's cone.
		In any case, when the car spinning innebitably slows down due to the strong sliding friction, the contact points will snap back to sticking and traction will be regained.
		
		To be honest, this might even be more accurate overall.
		I am very sure this will have a convergence behavior much closer to frictionless simulation than what we have now.

		Note2: We can do this with the history based contact points as well to avoid drifting.
